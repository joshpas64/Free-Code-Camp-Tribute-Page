An Honor to the Late Ajahn Chah
(1917 - 1992)

Ajahn Chah was one of the most revered and wise Buddhist monks of his time and had helped spread the knowledge and practices of Theravada Buddhism and the Thai Forest Tradition throughout the Western World.

The wise monk's start and early life

Ajahn Chah was born in a rural village in northeast Thailand in 1917 near Ubon. He lived a childhood and early life typical to those of other Thai farming boys of those days, including, as custom in some parts of the region, spending several years as a novice in a Buddhist monastery starting at age 9. He left, as some do, to return home and help his family on their farm. Eventually, he felt a draw back to the monastic Buddhist lifestyle, he reentered a Wat to be ordained as a Bhikku, a Buddhist monk. Upon the death of his father, he realized the transcience of life and instilled within him a desire to learn the essence of Buddha's teachings. It was at that point, he decided to live life as an ascetic, a pilgriming monk, learning teachings across various monasterious while traveling across Thailand. He eventually met Ajahn Mun Bhuridatto, a renowned forest meditation master of his time. At the time, he was having trouble learning how to put some extensive Buddhist sciptures into full practice. Ajahn Mun replied by stating

at their heart they are very simple. With mindfulness established, if it is seen everything that arises in the heart-mind: right there is the true path of practice.

stated from the venerable Ajahn Mun and taken from The teachings of Ajahn Chah
His time with Ajahn Mun brought great revelations to him. For the following seven years, practiced the austere Forest Tradition of an ascetic monk. Many times he traveled and meditated in jungles, caves, cremation grounds as well as to overcome certain fears he still held. For example, he traveled to animal infested jungles that could have tigers and cobras, where through the constant danger of death, he could ponder the meaning of life. Through meditating in a cremation ground, to challenge his fear of mortality. Through times alone during brutal and rainstorms, he learned and accepted the isolation and desolation of being a homeless monk.

Ajahn Chah's Contributions

Eventually Ajahn Chah was invited to settle into a forest grove around the village of his birth. His impeccable methods of teaching and meditation began to attract many monks, nuns, and commoners towards him. This eventually led to a large monastery being formed around the area. As his monastery grew in influence, words of his teaching style spread. In 1966, Ajahn Chah received his first western disciple, Sumedho Bhikku. The Bhikku had came to Ajahn Chah in order to find a teacher where he could fully in every aspect of the monastic lifestyle. Ajahn Chah welcomed him in, but insisted that he be given no special treatment, that he must practice and live as every other monk or nun in the monastery did. From then on, the number of foreign visitors began to increase at a steady rate. Since Sumedho Bhikku had gained enough knowledge and experience, he was permitted to teach where he and a group of other Western Bhikkus came to study in a forest near Wat Nong Pah Pong.

Wat Nong Pah Pong

There, with the help and encouragement of local villagers,they eventually built Wat Pah Nanachat(International Forest Monastery). A monastery that was run by and was run for English-speaking Buddhist monks. Through 1977 to 1980, Ajahn Chah, along with his closest of followers, would travel to Britain and across Europe to teach, helping to establish a monastery in Sussex and going on to continue teaching in Canada and the U.S.

Ajahn Chah's Qualities and Teaching Style

Ajahn Chah was known for a simple, yet deep and wise style of teaching. It would be said one should not be deceived by his simplicity for that

"It is only after we have heard something many times that suddenly our minds aree ripe and somehow the teaching takes on a much deeper meaning"
Passage from The Teachings of Ajahn Chah (xv)
Ajahn Chah was known for tailoring his sermons and teachings to the time and place he spoke of them, with consideration taken to whatever audience he was speaking to and whatever events may have transpired recently, always speaking truly and passionately from the heart with no notes or any rehearsals.
He endeavored to preserve a simple monastic lifestyle so that anyone could successfully practice the Dhamma in present-day. He was also noted for having an outstanding approach to meditation, simple but direct method of teaching, emphasizing practical application and a balanced attitude. His monks and nuns would undergo many tests of endurance, including long work projects, in order to enhance their resolve and patience and to even disrupt any attachment to tranquility for he saw surrending to the way things are and to build discipline. This style would attract large crowds of other monks, nuns, and common villagers at his first monastery, and eventually westerners as well, regardless of status or education level. His main goal could be considered as providing a path to liberation for his listeners. Even during health deterioration due to diabetes, he would use his body as a demonstration of the impermanence of all things and that people should learn to find refuge within themselves.

Death and Lasting Legacy

For 10 years, Ajahn Chah's health had rendered him unable to speak and bedridden. In 1992, he passed away on January 16 at 74 years old. He would leave behind a community of monasteries and followers that would span to and include areas in Thailand, Switzerland, New Zealand, Great Britain, the United States, Italy, Australia, France, and Canada. His funeral had over 1 million attendants, including the Thai royal family.

There is much literature and publishings in regards to his teachings, including a large translated collection of dhamma talks he gave over the years.

After Ajahn Chah had the question translated, he pondered for a moment and then started picking his nose in a very graphic and extended way. When everyone was rolling on the floor laughing and he was sure they definitely got the point, he pulled his finger out of his nose: 'There's nothing more to it than that, except what the mind adds to it.' Perhaps this story has been altered a bit in the telling, but it's still a good story."

  • "When you contemplate and see impermanence, suffering and notself, there will no longer be clinging to a self, a being, I or he or she.
